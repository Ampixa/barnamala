\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The DHCD dataset~\cite{acharya2015dhcd} has become the standard benchmark for
46-class handwritten Devanagari character recognition. Top-1 accuracies have
crowded into a narrow band above 99.7\,\%---a situation that, it turns out,
makes the benchmark harder to interpret than it first appears.
MallaNet~\cite{malla2025} reaches 99.71\,\% with a \textbf{17.32\,M}-parameter
convolutional architecture; Mishra~\cite{mishra2021} independently reports
99.72\,\% with a similarly large model.
At this level successive gains are measured in \emph{hundredths of a percent},
yet the models claiming them still carry parameter budgets far beyond what a
compact on-device recognizer can reasonably sustain.
So the question the field arguably needs answered is not which model ranks
first---it is whether the gap between large and small models reflects a real
performance difference or just measurement noise.

Existing DHCD papers leave that question unresolved.
Point accuracies dominate. When McNemar testing appears at all, it is used
selectively against weaker baselines, without testing the nearest competitor
and without reporting exact $p$-values.
With a test set of $n{=}13{,}800$ samples and accuracies near 99.7\,\%,
overlapping unpaired confidence intervals are essentially guaranteed---raw-accuracy
rankings alone carry little inferential weight.

The decisive question is \emph{paired}: on the \emph{same} test images, do two
models fail on \emph{different} examples?
We answer it with the exact McNemar test for every student--teacher pair we
evaluate, alongside Wilson confidence intervals for each model's accuracy.
Applying this systematically---rather than selectively---changes what the
leaderboard actually says.

The result is striking. A \textbf{1.11\,M}-parameter student reaches statistical
parity with MallaNet (McNemar $p=0.345$) using \textbf{15.6\(\times\)} fewer
parameters, and the same parity holds \emph{even without knowledge distillation}.
This turns out to matter more than expected: across distillation targets
(TTA-softened vs.\ clean no-TTA targets, and a supervised control) and teacher
ensemble size (9 vs.\ 15 teachers), \emph{no lever} produces a statistically
significant improvement.
Every model tested converges to the same \textbf{11-error intrinsic floor},
where DHCD's label noise and handwriting ambiguity appear to dominate.
The benchmark is saturated. Meaningful progress now requires either a harder
evaluation set or substantially different modelling assumptions.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{C1.}] \modelname{}---a compact student with \textbf{1.11\,M}
    parameters---achieves \textbf{99.73\,\%} on DHCD, the highest reported
    accuracy on the standard 46-class split, at a \textbf{15.6\(\times\)}
    smaller footprint than prior state-of-the-art.
    McNemar ($p=0.345$) confirms statistical parity with the nearest large
    baseline, and the student ties it \emph{even without distillation}.

  \item[\textbf{C2.}] Exact McNemar tests and Wilson CIs applied
    systematically (not selectively) across all configurations reveal
    that DHCD is \textbf{saturated}: every model shares the same
    \textbf{11-error intrinsic floor}.

  \item[\textbf{C3.}] We ablate distillation targets (TTA-softened vs.\ clean
    no-TTA targets vs.\ supervised control), teacher ensemble size (9 vs.\ 15
    teachers), and test-time augmentation.
    The ablations show which design choices matter and---once statistical noise
    is properly accounted for---which arguably do not.

  \item[\textbf{C4.}] Efficiency, transfer, and robustness profiling:
    \modelname{} is \textbf{15.6\(\times\)} smaller and faster than
    MallaNet, transfers to CMATERdb digits (zero-shot \textbf{76.6\%};
    fine-tuned \textbf{97.8\%}), and remains far more robust to common
    corruptions (mCA \textbf{75.7\%} vs.\ \textbf{38.7\%}).

  \item[\textbf{C5.}] Training code, model weights, distillation scripts, and
    evaluation harnesses are released at \repourl{}---fully reproducible
    artifacts for future work on Devanagari recognition and benchmark
    saturation.
\end{itemize}
