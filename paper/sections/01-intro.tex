\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The DHCD dataset~\cite{acharya2015dhcd} has emerged as the standard benchmark
for 46-class handwritten Devanagari character recognition, with reported
top-1 accuracies now clustered tightly above 99.7\,\%.
MallaNet~\cite{malla2025}, the current state-of-the-art, achieves 99.71\,\%
with a \textbf{17.32\,M}-parameter convolutional architecture;
Mishra~\cite{mishra2021} independently reports 99.72\,\% with a similarly
large model.
These results are strong, but they expose a structural tension: the
accuracy gains from one model generation to the next amount to
\emph{hundredths of a percent}, while parameter budgets remain an order of
magnitude larger than what a compact on-device model can afford.
Whether the residual gap between large and small models is real---or merely
measurement noise---has not been tested rigorously.

Prior work reports point accuracies, without paired statistical testing.
On a test set of $n{=}13{,}800$ samples at 99.7\,\% accuracy, overlapping
unpaired confidence intervals are unavoidable, and raw-accuracy rankings carry
no inferential weight.
The relevant question is \emph{paired}: given the same test images, do two
models make different errors?
This paper applies the exact McNemar test---the standard paired
binary-classification significance test---to every student--teacher pair we
evaluate, together with Wilson confidence intervals on each model's accuracy.
Prior DHCD work omitted this protocol; applying it changes the conclusions
substantially.

Our central finding is that a \textbf{1.11\,M}-parameter student network
reaches statistical parity with MallaNet (McNemar $p=0.345$) at
\textbf{15.6\(\times\)} fewer parameters, and that this parity holds
\emph{even without knowledge distillation}.
Ablations over distillation targets (TTA-softened vs.\ clean no-TTA targets, and a supervised control) and teacher ensemble size (9 vs.\ 15 teachers) find that \emph{no lever} yields a
statistically significant improvement: every model tested converges to the
same \textbf{11-error intrinsic floor}, beyond which DHCD's label noise and
handwriting ambiguity dominate.
The benchmark is therefore saturated; future progress requires either a
harder evaluation set or substantially different modelling assumptions.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{C1.}] A compact \modelname{} student (\textbf{1.11\,M}
    parameters) that matches MallaNet~\cite{malla2025} at 99.73\,\% on
    DHCD---\textbf{15.6\(\times\)} smaller and at statistical parity
    (McNemar $p=0.345$)---and ties it \emph{even without distillation}.

  \item[\textbf{C2.}] A rigorous paired evaluation protocol (exact McNemar
    tests, Wilson CIs) applied systematically across all configurations,
    showing that DHCD is \textbf{saturated} at an
    \textbf{11-error intrinsic floor} shared by every model.

  \item[\textbf{C3.}] Ablations over distillation targets (TTA-softened vs.\ clean no-TTA targets vs.\ supervised control), teacher ensemble size (9 vs.\ 15 teachers), and test-time augmentation, showing which design choices matter and which do not once statistical noise is properly controlled.

  \item[\textbf{C4.}] Efficiency, transfer, and robustness profiling:
    \modelname{} is \textbf{15.6\(\times\)} smaller and faster than
    MallaNet, transfers to CMATERdb digits (zero-shot \textbf{76.6\%};
    fine-tuned \textbf{97.8\%}), and remains far more robust to common
    corruptions (mCA \textbf{75.7\%} vs.\ \textbf{38.7\%}).

  \item[\textbf{C5.}] Fully reproducible artifacts---training code, model
    weights, distillation scripts, and evaluation harnesses---released at
    \repourl{} to facilitate future work on Devanagari recognition and
    benchmark saturation.
\end{itemize}
