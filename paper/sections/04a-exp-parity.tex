\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We use the standard Devanagari Handwritten Character Dataset
(DHCD)~\cite{acharya2015dhcd}, a 46-class benchmark (36 consonants and
10 numerals) for testing whether a much smaller model can match prior
high-accuracy systems.  The official split provides 78,200 training
images and 13,800 test images, each stored as a $32{\times}32$
greyscale PNG.

\paragraph{Reproducing the MallaNet baseline.}
For sample-level comparison with MallaNet, we re-implemented the
MallaNet architecture of~\cite{malla2025} from the published
description and reproduced the reported training procedure, including
the exact normalisation ($\text{mean}{=}0.5,\;\text{std}{=}0.5$) and
the class-index permutation implied by their data loader.  With seed~0,
this reproduction yields \textbf{40 errors} on the held-out test set,
or \textbf{99.7101\%} accuracy, matching the 99.71\% figure reported
in~\cite{malla2025}.  We use this checkpoint (seed 0) as the baseline
in all McNemar tests, with directly comparable per-sample correctness
vectors.

\subsection{Main Parity Result}
\label{sec:parity}

Table~\ref{tab:main} compares each \modelname{} configuration across
five independent seeds with the reproduced MallaNet baseline.

\begin{table}[t]\centering
\caption{Main result on DHCD test ($n{=}13{,}800$). Errors over 5
  seeds; McNemar vs.\ reproduced MallaNet (seed~0).}
\label{tab:main}
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}\toprule
Configuration & Params & Errors (5 seeds) & Mean$\pm$std & Acc \% & McNemar $p$ \\\midrule
\modelname, distilled (TTA)   & 1.11M & 34,39,35,39,36 & \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} & 99.735 & 0.345 \\
\modelname, distilled (clean) & 1.11M & 39,40,34,40,38 & 38.2$\pm$2.2 & 99.723 & 1.000 \\
\modelname, supervised        & 1.11M & 43,40,42,35,39 & 39.8$\pm$2.8 & 99.712 & 0.701 \\
MallaNet \cite{malla2025} (repro) & 17.32M & 40 & --- & 99.710 & --- \\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

In the \emph{primary} configuration (\textbf{distilled, TTA targets}),
the student learns from 9 teacher checkpoints (configurations A, B, C
$\times$ seeds 0--2), with soft labels from flip-averaged test-time
augmentation over each training image (Section~\ref{sec:kd}).  This
yields \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} errors (99.735\%) with a two-sided
McNemar $p = 0.345$, making the compact student statistically
indistinguishable from the reproduced MallaNet baseline at
$\alpha = 0.05$.  The best single seed reaches \textbf{34 errors}
(99.754\%), with a Wilson 95\% confidence interval of
\textbf{[99.656\%, 99.824\%]}.  At 1.11\,M parameters versus 17.32\,M
for MallaNet, the same accuracy regime is reached with a
\textbf{15.6$\times$} smaller model.

The \emph{ablation} row (distilled, clean targets) uses clean
single-pass logits from a 15-teacher pool instead of flip-averaged
targets; it obtains $p = 1.000$ against MallaNet, consistent with the
same accuracy level but no added benefit from TTA targets.  The
\emph{supervised control} — trained without any teacher signal — also
ties MallaNet ($p = 0.701$).  Thus, parity is not carried by knowledge
distillation alone; the compact architecture itself is sufficient to
match the larger model.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}
\label{sec:sota}

Table~\ref{tab:sota} places \modelname{} beside all published results
on the standard 46-class DHCD test split.  Its mean accuracy of
99.735\,\% is the highest reported on this split, but the top four
models are separated by only 0.07\,\%, well within sampling noise on a
13,800-image test set; the saturation finding (Section~\ref{sec:parity})
explains why.  McNemar tests require per-sample predictions, so they
are available only for MallaNet, the single prior model we
re-implemented.

  for the re-implemented MallaNet baseline (Table~\ref{tab:main}).}
\label{tab:sota}
\begin{tabular}{llrrl}\toprule

The result is about size, not a new accuracy ceiling.  \modelname{} is
the only model in this table with both a verified parameter budget
under 2\,M \emph{and} accuracy above 99.5\,\%.  The next lightest model
above 99.5\,\% is MallaNet at 17.32\,M parameters~\cite{malla2025}---a
\textbf{15.6$\times$} gap.  Prior compact models (Yadav et al.,
0.4\,M) fall 0.5\,\% below the saturation floor; much larger models
(Mishra et al., 39\,M) gain nothing over \modelname{} once the
benchmark is saturated.

This deployment gap does not change the accuracy conclusion.
\modelname{} requires \textbf{14.8$\times$} fewer multiply-accumulate
operations (164\,M vs.\ 2,434\,M MACs), is \textbf{15.6$\times$}
smaller on disk (4.4\,MB vs.\ 69.3\,MB), and occupies
\textbf{9.5$\times$} less peak activation memory (2.8\,MB vs.\
26.1\,MB).  CPU latency on a single thread is \textbf{9.5$\times$}
lower (8.28\,ms vs.\ 78.8\,ms), and single-image throughput is
\textbf{11.1$\times$} higher (151\,img/s vs.\ 13.6\,img/s).  The
comparison uses no quantisation or pruning; the 13,800-sample test set
still shows accuracy at parity with MallaNet.
