\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Dataset.}
All experiments use the Devanagari Handwritten Character Dataset
(DHCD)~\cite{acharya2015dhcd}, covering 46 character classes
(36 consonants and 10 numerals).  The official split provides
78,200 training images and 13,800 test images, each stored as a
$32{\times}32$ greyscale PNG.

\paragraph{Reproducing the MallaNet baseline.}
For statistical comparison, we needed a baseline with sample-level
predictions.  We re-implemented the MallaNet architecture
of~\cite{malla2025} from the published description and reproduced the
reported training procedure, including the exact normalisation
($\text{mean}{=}0.5,\;\text{std}{=}0.5$) and the class-index
permutation implied by their data loader.  Running this reproduction
with seed~0 yields \textbf{40 errors} on the held-out test
set — an accuracy of \textbf{99.7101\%}, in agreement with the
99.71\% figure reported in~\cite{malla2025}.  We use this reproduced
checkpoint (seed 0) as the baseline in all McNemar tests, keeping the
per-sample correctness vectors directly comparable.

\subsection{Main Parity Result}
\label{sec:parity}

Table~\ref{tab:main} shows error counts across five independent seeds
for three \modelname{} configurations and the MallaNet reproduction.

\begin{table}[t]\centering
\caption{Main result on DHCD test ($n{=}13{,}800$). Errors over 5
  seeds; McNemar vs.\ reproduced MallaNet (seed~0).}
\label{tab:main}
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}\toprule
Configuration & Params & Errors (5 seeds) & Mean$\pm$std & Acc \% & McNemar $p$ \\\midrule
\modelname, distilled (TTA)   & 1.11M & 34,39,35,39,36 & \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} & 99.735 & 0.345 \\
\modelname, distilled (clean) & 1.11M & 39,40,34,40,38 & 38.2$\pm$2.2 & 99.723 & 1.000 \\
\modelname, supervised        & 1.11M & 43,40,42,35,39 & 39.8$\pm$2.8 & 99.712 & 0.701 \\
MallaNet \cite{malla2025} (repro) & 17.32M & 40 & --- & 99.710 & --- \\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

In the \emph{primary} configuration (\textbf{distilled, TTA targets}),
the student is distilled from 9 teacher checkpoints
(configurations A, B, C $\times$ seeds 0--2) whose soft labels are
produced with flip-averaged test-time augmentation over each training
image, as described in Section~\ref{sec:kd}.  This row achieves a
mean of \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} errors (99.735\%) with a two-sided
McNemar $p = 0.345$, indicating statistical parity with the
reproduced MallaNet baseline at $\alpha = 0.05$.
The best single seed reaches \textbf{34 errors} (99.754\%), with a
Wilson 95\% confidence interval of \textbf{[99.656\%, 99.824\%]}.
At 1.11\,M parameters versus 17.32\,M for MallaNet, this represents
a \textbf{15.6$\times$} reduction in model size at matched accuracy.

The \emph{ablation} row (distilled, clean targets) uses clean
single-pass logits drawn from a 15-teacher pool instead of
flip-averaged targets; it obtains $p = 1.000$ against MallaNet,
consistent with the same accuracy level but no benefit from TTA
targets.  The
\emph{supervised control} — trained without any
teacher signal — also ties MallaNet ($p = 0.701$).  The parity
result is therefore not dependent on knowledge distillation; the
compact architecture alone is sufficient to match the larger model.

\subsection{Efficiency}
\label{sec:efficiency}

Table~\ref{tab:eff} and Figure~\ref{fig:frontier} summarise the
computational gap between \modelname{} and MallaNet.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/efficiency_frontier.pdf}
  \caption{Accuracy–parameter trade-off on DHCD.
    \modelname{} (1.11\,M) matches MallaNet (17.32\,M) accuracy at
    15.6$\times$ fewer parameters.
    Each marker for \modelname{} is an independent seed.}
  \label{fig:frontier}

\modelname{} requires \textbf{14.8$\times$} fewer multiply-accumulate
operations (164\,M vs.\ 2,434\,M MACs), is \textbf{15.6$\times$}
smaller on disk (4.4\,MB vs.\ 69.3\,MB), and occupies \textbf{9.5$\times$}
less peak activation memory (2.8\,MB vs.\ 26.1\,MB).  CPU latency
on a single thread is \textbf{9.5$\times$} lower (8.28\,ms vs.\
78.8\,ms), and single-image throughput is \textbf{11.1$\times$}
higher (151\,img/s vs.\ 13.6\,img/s).  The comparison uses no
quantisation or pruning; accuracy remains at parity with MallaNet on
the 13,800-sample test set.
