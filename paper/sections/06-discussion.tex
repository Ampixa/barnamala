\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Scope of transfer results.}
The transfer experiment (Section~\ref{sec:transfer}) is deliberately
narrow: it uses only the \textbf{digit subset} (10 of 46 DHCD classes).
That choice reflects the available data, not a design preference.
Extending the test to the full 36-consonant inventory would require a
genuinely independent target set with DHCD's label taxonomy; the apparent
public candidates — principally the Acharya~\cite{acharya2015dhcd} and
Pant datasets — are same-provenance splits of DHCD itself.  Treating them
as held-out targets would reintroduce training-evaluation contamination.
This paper can therefore make transferability claims for
digits, but not for consonants.  That limitation is informative: the
Devanagari recognition community lacks the independent held-out corpora
needed to test whether gains survive real provenance shifts.  The digit
result, where the compact model outperforms a supervised baseline by
\textbf{+14\,pp} under domain shift, is evidence that those benchmarks are
worth building, not a substitute for them.

\paragraph{Single-benchmark evaluation.}
The consonant-level accuracy results rest on a single held-out split of
DHCD.  The McNemar test is still useful: it compares models on the same
examples and controls for within-split variability, but it cannot answer
whether the ordering holds when data provenance changes.  The saturation
claim is therefore tied to DHCD's particular conditions, including its
$32{\times}32$ resolution, concentrated label noise, and writer pool.  A
cleaner or more diverse consonant benchmark might expose capacity
differences that DHCD no longer reveals.  This reflects the public
benchmark landscape, not a peculiarity of the present experiment.  The
right interpretation is narrow but useful: DHCD appears saturated at this
resolution and provenance, while Devanagari recognition as a broader
problem remains less settled.

\paragraph{Efficiency versus accuracy.}
The \textbf{15.6$\times$} parameter reduction and \textbf{9.5$\times$}
latency improvement matter because they do not trade away DHCD accuracy.
At the benchmark's current saturation point, the remaining mistakes appear
governed by data more than model capacity, so a larger network has little
room to justify its cost.  In that setting, compactness is the appropriate
response to the evidence, not a concession.  A more
discriminative benchmark, especially one where additional capacity could
push below the 11-error floor, might change the efficiency trade-off.
These results establish a deployment argument for DHCD-like conditions,
while leaving open whether the same argument holds once the benchmark
itself stops being the limiting factor.
