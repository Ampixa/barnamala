\subsection{Distillation and Ensemble Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation-kd}

\paragraph{Effect of knowledge distillation and target cleanliness.}
Table~\ref{tab:main} includes three rows that isolate the contribution of
knowledge distillation and the choice of teacher-target generation scheme.
In the primary configuration, \emph{distilled (TTA) targets}, the student is
trained from soft labels generated by averaging each teacher's predictions over
a horizontal-flip pair of each training image (see Section~\ref{sec:kd});
this yields a mean of \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} errors.
For \emph{distilled (clean) targets}, soft labels come from a single clean
forward pass through a larger 15-teacher pool with no flip averaging; this
setting yields \textbf{38.2$\pm$2.2} errors.
The \emph{supervised control}, trained with no teacher signal at all, obtains
\textbf{39.8$\pm$2.8} errors.

Distillation gives only a small reduction of roughly 3 errors relative to the
supervised baseline (36.6 vs.\ 39.8), and none of the three McNemar $p$-values
approach $0.05$ (Table~\ref{tab:main}).  The clean-target and TTA-target
distilled students are statistically indistinguishable (38.2 vs.\ 36.6, with
substantial seed overlap in the error distributions): flip-averaged teacher
targets do not improve on clean single-pass targets.  The modest distillation
benefit is therefore \emph{insensitive} to the cleanliness of the soft labels.
We keep the smaller 9-teacher pool with flip-averaged targets as the primary
configuration for simplicity, but the result does not depend on that choice.

\paragraph{Teacher ensemble size.}
We also test whether scaling the teacher pool changes the ensemble error rate
when the ensemble is used as a \emph{direct predictor} on the test set
(distinct from its role in generating training-time soft targets).
Table~\ref{tab:ensemble} shows the outcome.

Individual teachers span a wide range: clean per-model error counts are
27, 28, 30, 30, 32, 34, 36, 37, 37, 39, 39, 40, 41, 42, and 43.
Both the 9-teacher and the 15-teacher majority-vote pools plateau at

against the reproduced MallaNet baseline.  This is a clear negative result:
adding six more teachers provides no improvement.

\subsection{Flip-TTA at Test Time}
\label{sec:flip-tta}

The third row of Table~\ref{tab:ensemble} applies horizontal-flip test-time
augmentation to the 9-teacher ensemble at \emph{evaluation time} — averaging
each teacher's predictions over the original and horizontally-flipped test
image before taking the majority vote.  This lowers ensemble errors to
\textbf{28} ($p = 0.029$), the only entry in our study that crosses the 0.05
threshold.

We do not treat this as a positive result, for two reasons.

First, flip-TTA is semantically invalid for Devanagari.  Horizontal reflection
produces characters that do not exist in the script, and the training
distribution deliberately excludes horizontal flips (Section~\ref{sec:training}).
Averaging predictions over that out-of-distribution augmentation introduces a
systematic bias with data-dependent, uncontrolled direction.

The apparent gain is also fragile.  When flip-TTA is applied to
\emph{individual} teachers rather than the ensemble, errors increase
dramatically — reaching \textbf{88} and \textbf{102} on the worst-affected
checkpoints, up from clean baselines of 30--43.  The ensemble partially
masks this variance because individual teachers' errors correlate in ways that
happen to cancel on this particular test split.  The McNemar margin itself is
only 2 samples (30 vs.\ 28 errors) on a test set of 13,800 images, giving a
Wilson interval that nearly straddles parity.

The important distinction is that the student distillation targets in
Section~\ref{sec:kd} are \emph{also} generated with flip-averaging — but over
\emph{training} images, where the flip is applied to the source document being
transcribed, not to a held-out test sample.  That use is a target-smoothing
device during training, not a prediction strategy, and is not affected by the
invalidity of flip-TTA as a test-time predictor.  The finding here invalidates
flip augmentation when it is used to produce final predictions on the test set.
We therefore exclude the $p = 0.029$ result from the set of defensible
conclusions and report the clean ensemble baselines
(30 errors) as the upper-bound reference for teacher performance.
