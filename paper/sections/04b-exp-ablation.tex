\subsection{Distillation and Ensemble Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation-kd}

\paragraph{Effect of knowledge distillation and target cleanliness.}
Table~\ref{tab:main} separates two questions: whether the student benefits
from a teacher at all, and whether the way we generate teacher targets matters.
In the primary configuration, \emph{distilled (TTA) targets}, the student learns
from soft labels generated by averaging each teacher's predictions over a
horizontal-flip pair of each training image (see Section~\ref{sec:kd}); it
ends at \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} errors.  With \emph{distilled (clean) targets},
the soft labels come from a single clean forward pass through a larger
15-teacher pool with no flip averaging, and the student reaches
\textbf{38.2$\pm$2.2} errors.  The \emph{supervised control}, trained with no
teacher signal at all, obtains \textbf{39.8$\pm$2.8} errors.

Three errors separate the best distilled student from the supervised
baseline---modest, and none of the three McNemar $p$-values approach $0.05$
(Table~\ref{tab:main}).  Distillation helps, but only a little.  What is
perhaps more interesting is how little the target recipe matters: the
clean-target and TTA-target students are statistically indistinguishable (38.2
vs.\ 36.6, with substantial seed overlap in the error distributions).
Flip-averaged teacher targets do not outperform a clean single-pass forward
pass through the same teacher.  The useful signal is the teacher itself, not
the extra target cleanup.  We keep the smaller 9-teacher pool with
flip-averaged targets as the primary configuration for simplicity, but the
result does not depend on that choice.

\paragraph{Teacher ensemble size.}
A natural follow-up: is the plateau at 30 errors a ceiling imposed by having
only 9 teachers?  To check, we ran the ensemble as a \emph{direct predictor}
on the test set---not as a source of training-time soft targets---and
compared 9 teachers against 15.  The answer from Table~\ref{tab:ensemble} is
blunt: more teachers do not move the ceiling.

\begin{table}[t]\centering
\caption{Teacher ensembles on DHCD test ($n{=}13{,}800$). Rows 1--2: scaling from 9 to 15 teachers does not move the 30-error plateau. Row 3: adding flip-TTA crosses $p{<}0.05$ but is methodologically invalid (Section~\ref{sec:flip-tta}).}\label{tab:ensemble}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}\toprule
Ensemble & Errors & Acc\,\% & McNemar $p$ & $p{<}0.05$? \\\midrule
9 teachers, clean     & 30 & 99.783 & 0.0755 & no \\
15 teachers, clean    & 30 & 99.783 & 0.0755 & no \\
9 teachers, flip-TTA  & 28 & 99.797 & 0.029  & fragile \\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}\end{table}

Individual teachers disagree enough to make the test meaningful---clean
per-model error counts span 27--43---yet both the 9-teacher and 15-teacher
majority-vote pools plateau at \textbf{30 errors} (99.783\%), with an
identical McNemar $p = 0.0755$.  Ensemble diversity is not the bottleneck:
the remaining errors sit in an irreducible floor where additional models vote
the same wrong answer.

\subsection{Flip-TTA at Test Time}
\label{sec:flip-tta}

Averaging each teacher's logits over the original and horizontally-flipped test
image drops errors to \textbf{28} ($p = 0.029$), the only result in our study
that crosses the 0.05 threshold; we do not treat this as a positive result.
Flip-TTA is semantically invalid for Devanagari: horizontal reflection produces
characters absent from the script, and the training distribution deliberately
excludes such flips (Section~\ref{sec:training}).
The gain is also fragile: applied to \emph{individual} teachers, flip-TTA
raises errors to \textbf{88} and \textbf{102} on the worst-affected checkpoints,
up from clean baselines of 30--43, with the ensemble masking this variance only
by accident on this split.
Note that the student's training-time targets (Section~\ref{sec:kd}) also use
flip-averaging, but over \emph{training} images as a target-smoothing device---a
distinct use that is unaffected by the invalidity of flip-TTA as a test-time
predictor.
We therefore exclude the $p = 0.029$ result and report the 30-error clean
ensemble as the upper-bound reference for teacher performance.
