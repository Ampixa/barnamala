\subsection{Distillation and Ensemble Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation-kd}

\paragraph{Effect of knowledge distillation and target cleanliness.}
Table~\ref{tab:main} separates two questions: whether the student benefits
from a teacher at all, and whether the way we generate teacher targets matters.
In the primary configuration, \emph{distilled (TTA) targets}, the student learns
from soft labels generated by averaging each teacher's predictions over a
horizontal-flip pair of each training image (see Section~\ref{sec:kd}); it
ends at \textbf{36.6$\pm$2.1} errors.  With \emph{distilled (clean) targets},
the soft labels come from a single clean forward pass through a larger
15-teacher pool with no flip averaging, and the student reaches
\textbf{38.2$\pm$2.2} errors.  The \emph{supervised control}, trained with no
teacher signal at all, obtains \textbf{39.8$\pm$2.8} errors.

Three errors separate the best distilled student from the supervised
baseline---modest, and none of the three McNemar $p$-values approach $0.05$
(Table~\ref{tab:main}).  Distillation helps, but only a little.  What is
perhaps more interesting is how little the target recipe matters: the
clean-target and TTA-target students are statistically indistinguishable (38.2
vs.\ 36.6, with substantial seed overlap in the error distributions).
Flip-averaged teacher targets do not outperform a clean single-pass forward
pass through the same teacher.  The useful signal is the teacher itself, not
the extra target cleanup.  We keep the smaller 9-teacher pool with
flip-averaged targets as the primary configuration for simplicity, but the
result does not depend on that choice.

\paragraph{Teacher ensemble size.}
A natural follow-up: is the plateau at 30 errors a ceiling imposed by having
only 9 teachers?  To check, we ran the ensemble as a \emph{direct predictor}
on the test set---not as a source of training-time soft targets---and
compared 9 teachers against 15.  The answer from Table~\ref{tab:ensemble} is
blunt: more teachers do not move the ceiling.

It is worth pausing on why this negative result is as clean as it is.  The
individual teachers still disagree enough to make the test meaningful---clean
per-model error counts are 27, 28, 30, 30, 32, 34, 36, 37, 37, 39, 39, 40,
41, 42, and 43---so the vote is not trivially saturated by a homogeneous pool.
Yet both the 9-teacher and the 15-teacher majority-vote pools plateau at
\textbf{30 errors} (99.783\%), with an identical McNemar $p = 0.0755$ against
the reproduced MallaNet baseline.  This is a cleaner negative result than it
might appear: ensemble diversity is not the bottleneck.  The remaining 30
errors sit in an irreducible floor where additional models vote the same wrong
answer, not in the tail of model-specific variance that more teachers would
correct.

\subsection{Flip-TTA at Test Time}
\label{sec:flip-tta}

Averaging each teacher's predictions over the original and horizontally-flipped
test image---before taking the majority vote---is what the third row of
Table~\ref{tab:ensemble} tests.  Errors drop to \textbf{28} ($p = 0.029$),
the only entry in our study that crosses the 0.05 threshold.

We do not treat this as a positive result, for two reasons.

First, flip-TTA is semantically invalid for Devanagari.  Horizontal reflection
produces characters that do not exist in the script, and the training
distribution deliberately excludes horizontal flips (Section~\ref{sec:training}).
Averaging predictions over that out-of-distribution augmentation introduces a
systematic bias with data-dependent, uncontrolled direction.

The apparent gain is also fragile.  When flip-TTA is applied to
\emph{individual} teachers rather than the ensemble, errors increase
dramatically, reaching \textbf{88} and \textbf{102} on the worst-affected
checkpoints, up from clean baselines of 30--43.  The ensemble partially
masks this variance because individual teachers' errors correlate in ways that
happen to cancel on this particular test split---and the McNemar margin itself
is only 2 samples (30 vs.\ 28 errors) on a test set of 13,800 images, giving a
Wilson interval that nearly straddles parity.

One detail deserves clarification.  The student distillation targets in
Section~\ref{sec:kd} are \emph{also} generated with flip-averaging, but over
\emph{training} images, where the flip is applied to the source document being
transcribed, not to a held-out test sample.  That use is a target-smoothing
device during training, not a prediction strategy, and is not affected by the
invalidity of flip-TTA as a test-time predictor.  The finding here invalidates
flip augmentation when it is used to produce final predictions on the test set.
We therefore exclude the $p = 0.029$ result from the set of defensible
conclusions and report the clean ensemble baselines
(30 errors) as the upper-bound reference for teacher performance.
