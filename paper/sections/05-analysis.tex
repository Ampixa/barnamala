\section{Analysis: Why DHCD is Saturated}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The Shared Error Floor}

DHCD now has a shared error floor, not just hard examples: across all 15
\modelname{} students and the reproduced MallaNet baseline~\cite{malla2025},
\textbf{11 images} defeat every model.  \emph{No} configuration classifies
these samples correctly, regardless of architecture, training strategy, or
random seed.  Figure~\ref{fig:floor} visualises the cumulative
error-sharing distribution: errors fall sharply from the universal
11-image floor into a sparse tail of model-specific misses, concentrating
the remaining failure mass in this irreducible core.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/error_floor.pdf}
  \caption{%
    Number of test images missed by exactly $k$ of the 16 models (15
    \modelname{} students $+$ MallaNet).
    Eleven images are missed by all 16 models simultaneously, forming an
    irreducible error floor; the remaining errors distribute sparsely
    across individual models.
  }
  \label{fig:floor}
\end{figure}

When the same 11-image floor catches both a 1.11\,M-parameter compact
student and a baseline fifteen times its size, the problem is more likely
in the data than in the models: these samples are \emph{mislabelled or
fundamentally ambiguous} in DHCD, not hard examples a better model could
eventually learn.  Outside this floor, most errors are model-specific---the
mean pairwise Jaccard similarity of error sets across models is
$\mathbf{0.48}$, which suggests seeds and configurations shuffle which
images fall in the error tail without reliably reducing it.  Ensembling
or adding training seeds reduces \emph{variance}, not the underlying
\emph{bias} imposed by the floor.

\subsection{Confusable Character Pairs}

Script-level collisions dominate remaining mistakes.  Table~\ref{tab:confusions} lists
the top distilled-student confusion pairs aggregated across the 10
distilled seeds.  \textbf{ba / waw} leads with 17 cross-confusions, followed by
\textbf{waw / tabala} (16) and \textbf{tra / ba} (15).  Roughly 48 errors
across the distilled student pool trace back to just these three pairs.
The \textbf{dha / gha} confusion (13 instances) comes from
near-identical looped ascenders separated only by a subtle curve in the
lower body of the glyph; the \textbf{da / dhaa} pair (9 instances)
turns on a single horizontal hairline.

What these pairs expose, it seems, is a resolution limit: the confusable glyphs differ by
strokes finer than a few pixels at $32{\times}32$ resolution.  Resolving
them would require higher-resolution inputs or richer annotation marking
which specific stroke is present; DHCD provides neither.

\subsection{Significance Frontier}
\label{sec:sig-frontier}

The McNemar test shows that statistical significance on DHCD is separated
from parity by only a handful of disputed images: it converts accuracy
into disagreement counts rather than raw totals, making the significance
frontier strikingly narrow.  Under the exact two-sided
test~\cite{mcnemar1947} at $\alpha = 0.05$, crossing from
statistical parity to a demonstrable improvement over the reproduced
MallaNet baseline (40 errors) requires at most \textbf{25 errors} on the
same 13,800-sample test set (corresponding to $\geq$99.82\% accuracy) ---
fewer than 15 additional correct classifications separating inconclusive
from significant.  A comfortable margin would require $\leq$\textbf{20
errors} ($\geq$99.855\%), a target that demands near-perfect resolution of
the ambiguous-glyph pairs in Table~\ref{tab:confusions}.

Every configuration tested stops just short of this frontier.  The best
single result is \textbf{28 errors}: the 9-teacher ensemble with
(semantically invalid) flip-TTA applied at test time, a result excluded
on methodological grounds (Section~\ref{sec:flip-tta}).  The best
defensible single seed reaches \textbf{34 errors}; the best ensemble
without flip-TTA reaches \textbf{30 errors}.  Both sit within the parity
region, separated from significance not by a rounding issue but by the
11-image irreducible floor itself: eliminating the floor would put even
the weaker configurations inside the frontier.

\textbf{No lever tested in this study achieved a statistically
significant improvement over MallaNet.}  Scaling the teacher pool from 9
to 15, applying TTA-softened distillation targets, and running five
independent seeds all leave configurations outside the significance
frontier.  This is not a failure of experimental design---it defines a
saturated benchmark.  The significance frontier is not a line researchers
are approaching asymptotically, but a wall built from mislabelled images
and inherently ambiguous glyphs.

\subsection{Calibration}

Calibration confirms residual errors are confident failures, not ordinary
uncertainty.  Expected Calibration Error (ECE)~\cite{guo2017calibration},
measured with 15 equal-width bins on the held-out DHCD test set, falls in
the range \textbf{0.13--0.16} across all \modelname{} student
configurations.  Unremarkable values for softmax classifiers without
post-hoc temperature scaling---but they tell a consistent story:
moderate over-confidence concentrated at the extremes of the confidence
distribution, where models assign very high probabilities to characters
they have seen thousands of times in training.  One interpretation is
that this pattern reflects training on a near-clean benchmark whose label
noise is confined to the 11-image floor rather than diffused across the
full class distribution.

Two features of the calibration landscape stand out.  ECE does
not co-vary with accuracy across seeds: better seeds are not better
calibrated, and worse seeds are not systematically over-confident in
distinct ways.  Calibration is essentially uniform while accuracy varies
within a narrow band.  Then there is the floor itself: the 11 floor images
sit at the tails of the confidence distribution regardless of seed---models
are confidently wrong on these samples, not uncertain.  Both observations
point to the same conclusion: the error floor is arguably a data-level
property imposed by the images themselves, not a model-level property that
architectural or training choices can correct.
