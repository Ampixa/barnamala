\section{Analysis: Why DHCD is Saturated}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The Shared Error Floor}

DHCD now has a shared error floor, not just hard examples: across all 15
\modelname{} students and the reproduced baseline~\cite{malla2025},
\textbf{11 images} defeat every model.  \emph{No} configuration classifies
these samples correctly---architecture and training strategy make no difference.

When the same 11-image floor catches both a 1.11\,M-parameter compact
student and a baseline fifteen times its size, the problem is in the data.
Outside this floor, errors are model-specific (mean pairwise Jaccard
similarity $\mathbf{0.48}$): seeds shuffle which images fail, but
ensembling or adding seeds reduces \emph{variance}, not the \emph{bias} the
floor imposes.

\subsection{Confusable Character Pairs}

Script-level collisions dominate remaining mistakes.  Table~\ref{tab:confusions} lists
the top distilled-student confusion pairs aggregated across the 10
distilled seeds.  \textbf{ba / waw} leads with 17 cross-confusions.
\textbf{waw / tabala} follows at 16 and \textbf{tra / ba} at 15, with
\textbf{dha / gha} (13) and \textbf{da / dhaa} (9) completing the top five.

At $32{\times}32$ pixels, the distinguishing strokes in these pairs span
only a few pixels---sometimes fewer.  DHCD provides neither
higher-resolution inputs nor stroke-level annotation, so the information
needed to resolve them is simply absent from the images.

\subsection{Significance Frontier}
\label{sec:sig-frontier}

Under the exact two-sided McNemar test~\cite{mcnemar1947} at $\alpha = 0.05$,
crossing from statistical parity to a demonstrable improvement over the
reproduced baseline (40 errors) requires at most \textbf{25 errors}
($\geq$99.82\% accuracy)---fewer than 15 additional correct classifications
separate inconclusive from significant.  Reaching a comfortable margin means
$\leq$\textbf{20 errors} ($\geq$99.855\%), which in turn means near-perfect
resolution of every ambiguous-glyph pair in Table~\ref{tab:confusions}.

Every configuration tested stops just short. \textbf{28 errors} is the best
single result: the 9-teacher ensemble with (semantically invalid) flip-TTA
applied at test time, excluded on methodological grounds
(Section~\ref{sec:flip-tta}).  The best defensible single seed reaches
\textbf{34 errors}; the clean ensemble without flip-TTA reaches \textbf{30 errors}.
Both sit within the parity region, pinned there by the 11-image irreducible
floor.

\textbf{No lever tested in this study achieved a statistically
significant improvement over the baseline.}  Scaling the teacher pool from 9
to 15 changed nothing.  TTA-softened distillation targets and five independent
seeds left all configurations outside the significance frontier.  This is not
a failure of experimental design---it defines a saturated benchmark.  The
frontier is a wall built from mislabelled images and inherently ambiguous
glyphs, not a line being approached asymptotically.


\subsection{Calibration}

Expected Calibration Error (ECE)~\cite{guo2017calibration} falls in the
range \textbf{0.13--0.16} across all \modelname{} configurations---unremarkable
for softmax classifiers without post-hoc temperature scaling.  ECE does not
co-vary with accuracy across seeds: better seeds are not better calibrated.
The 11 floor images are confidently wrong on every seed, not merely uncertain;
models assign high probability to an incorrect class regardless of how the
student was trained.
