\section{Analysis: Why DHCD is Saturated}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The Shared Error Floor}

Across all 15 \modelname{} students and the reproduced MallaNet
baseline~\cite{malla2025}, the same core of \textbf{11 images} defeats
every model.  \emph{No} configuration classifies these samples correctly,
regardless of architecture, training strategy, or random seed.
Figure~\ref{fig:floor} visualises the cumulative error-sharing
distribution: the steep drop from the universal 11-image floor into the
tail of model-specific errors shows that most residual errors are
concentrated in this irreducible core.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/error_floor.pdf}
  \caption{%
    Number of test images missed by exactly $k$ of the 16 models (15
    \modelname{} students $+$ MallaNet).
    Eleven images are missed by all 16 models simultaneously, forming an
    irreducible error floor; the remaining errors distribute sparsely
    across individual models.
  }
  \label{fig:floor}
\end{figure}

An 11-image floor shared by a 1.11\,M-parameter compact student and a
17.32\,M-parameter baseline points to samples that are \emph{mislabelled
or fundamentally ambiguous} in DHCD, rather than representative hard
examples that a better model could eventually learn.  Outside this floor,
most errors are model-specific: the mean pairwise Jaccard similarity of
error sets across models is
$\mathbf{0.48}$, indicating that seeds and configurations shuffle which
images fall in the error tail without reliably reducing it.  Ensembling
or adding training seeds reduces \emph{variance}; it does not address the
underlying \emph{bias} imposed by the floor.

\subsection{Confusable Character Pairs}

Table~\ref{tab:confusions} lists the top confusion pairs from the
distilled-student error analysis, aggregated across the 10 distilled seeds.
\textbf{ba / waw} leads with 17 cross-confusions, followed by
\textbf{waw / tabala} (16) and \textbf{tra / ba} (15).  Together these
three pairs account for roughly 48 errors across the distilled student
pool.  The \textbf{dha / gha} confusion (13 instances) arises from
near-identical looped ascenders that differ only by a subtle curve in the
lower body of the glyph; the \textbf{da / dhaa} pair (9 instances)
involves glyphs distinguished by a single horizontal hairline.

These pairs reflect structural properties of the Devanagari script.  The
confusable glyphs differ by strokes finer than a few pixels at
$32{\times}32$ resolution.  Resolving them would require
higher-resolution inputs or richer annotation marking which specific
stroke is present; DHCD provides neither.

\subsection{Significance Frontier}
\label{sec:sig-frontier}

Under the exact two-sided McNemar test~\cite{mcnemar1947} at
$\alpha = 0.05$, a statistically significant improvement over the
reproduced MallaNet baseline (40 errors) requires at most \textbf{25
errors} on the same 13,800-sample test set (corresponding to
$\geq$99.82\% accuracy).  A comfortable margin would require
$\leq$\textbf{20 errors} ($\geq$99.855\%).
The best single result is \textbf{28 errors}: the 9-teacher ensemble with
(semantically invalid) flip-TTA applied at test time, a result excluded
on methodological grounds (Section~\ref{sec:flip-tta}).  The best
defensible single seed reaches \textbf{34 errors}; the best ensemble
without flip-TTA reaches \textbf{30 errors}.

\textbf{No lever tested in this study achieved a statistically
significant improvement over MallaNet.}  Scaling the teacher pool from 9
to 15, applying TTA-softened distillation targets, and running five
independent seeds all leave the configurations outside the significance
frontier.  This is not a failure of experimental design; it is the
defining property of a saturated benchmark.

\subsection{Calibration}

Expected Calibration Error (ECE)~\cite{guo2017calibration}, measured
with 15 equal-width bins on the held-out DHCD test set, falls in the
range \textbf{0.13--0.16} across all \modelname{} student configurations.
These ECE values are typical of softmax classifiers without post-hoc
temperature scaling.  They indicate moderate over-confidence, consistent
with training on a near-clean benchmark whose label noise is concentrated
in the ambiguous floor images rather than spread across the distribution.
Calibration remains stable across seeds and does not co-vary with
accuracy, reinforcing the conclusion that the error floor is a data-level
property rather than a model-level property.
