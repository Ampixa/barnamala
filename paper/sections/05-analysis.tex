\section{Analysis: Why DHCD is Saturated}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The Shared Error Floor}

DHCD now has a shared error floor instead of just a hard example. All 15 \modelname{} students and the replicated baseline ~\cite{malla2025}, \textbf{11 images} broke all models. The \emph{No} configuration classifies these samples correctly. There is no difference in architecture or training strategy.

If the same 11 image floors capture both a compact Student of 1.11\,M parameters and a baseline 15 times its size, the problem lies in the data. Outside this floor, the error is model-specific (average pairwise Jaccard similarity $\mathbf{0.48}$). Seeds shuffle which images fail, but the addition of ensembles or seeds reduces \emph{variance} rather than \emph{bias} imposed by the floor.

\subsection{Confusable Character Pairs}

Script-level conflicts account for most of the remaining mistakes. Table ~\ref{tab:confusions} lists the top distillation-Student confusion pairs aggregated across the 10 distillation seeds. \textbf{ba / waw} is a lead with 17 mutual confusions. \textbf{waw / tabala} is in 16th place, \textbf{tra / ba} is in 15th place, and \textbf{dha / gha} (13) and \textbf{da / dhaa} (9) make up the top five.

In $32{\times}32$ pixels, the characteristic strokes of these pairs span just a few pixels, sometimes less. DHCD provides neither high-resolution input nor stroke-level annotations, so the information needed to resolve them is not present in the image.

\subsection{Significance Frontier}
\label{sec:sig-frontier}

Exact two-sided McNemar test~\cite{mcnemar1947} over $\alpha = 0.05$ requires at most \textbf{25 errors} ($\geq$99.82\% precision) to lead to a clear improvement over the reproduced baseline (40 errors) from statistical parity. There are fewer than 15 additional correct classifications that distinguish between inconclusive and important classifications. Reaching a comfortable margin means $\leq$\textbf{20 errors} ($\geq$99.855\%). This means that all ambiguous glyph pairs in Table~\ref{tab:confusions} are resolved almost perfectly.

All tested configurations stop immediately. \textbf{28 errors} is the single best result. 9-teacher ensemble, including the (semantically invalid) flip-TTA that was applied during testing, but is excluded for methodological reasons (section ~\ref{sec:flip-tta}). The most defensible single seed reaches \textbf{34 errors}. Clean ensemble without flips - TTA reaches \textbf{30 errors}. Both are located within the parity region and are fixed by an irreducible floor of 11 images.

\textbf{No lever tested in this study achieved a statistically
significant improvement over the baseline.} Increasing the number of teachers from 9 to 15 did not change anything. With the distillation target softened in TTA and five independent seeds, all configurations remained outside significance frontier. This is not a failure of experimental design. Define a saturated benchmark. The frontier is a wall built from mislabeled images and inherently ambiguous glyphs, not an asymptotically approaching line.


\subsection{Calibration}

Expected Calibration Error (ECE)~\cite{guo2017calibration} falls within the range of \textbf{0.13--0.16} across all \modelname{} configurations. It is not noticeable for softmax classifiers without post-hoc temperature scaling. ECE does not covary in accuracy between seeds. A better seed will not calibrate better. The 11th floor image is not just uncertain, it is definitely wrong at every seed. The model assigns a high probability to the incorrect class, regardless of how the student is trained.
