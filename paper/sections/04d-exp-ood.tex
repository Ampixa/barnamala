\subsection{Cross-Dataset Generalisation}
\label{sec:ood}

Both models are evaluated zero-shot on two independently collected Devanagari datasets---different writers, scanner workflows, and collection years---with no fine-tuning applied.

\paragraph{NHCD (Pant 2012).}
The Nepali Handwritten Character Dataset~\cite{pant2012nhcd} covers the same 46-class inventory in $28{\times}28$ JPEG images; we polarity-invert and bilinearly resize to $32{\times}32$ before evaluation.
\modelname{} achieves \textbf{78.92\%} zero-shot
(95.80\% on digits, 72.33\% on consonants).
The baseline~\cite{malla2025} collapses to \textbf{23.99\%}
(58.85\% on digits, 10.38\% on consonants)---a \textbf{55\,pp gap} despite
identical in-distribution performance on DHCD, with the +62\,pp consonant
disparity being the most striking number.

One systematic error cluster is linguistically informative.
For both models, the four dental/retroflex consonant pairs
(ta/\d{t}a, tha/\d{t}ha, da/\d{d}a, dha/\d{d}ha) produce
near-zero accuracy with systematic cross-class confusion.
These pairs are visually distinguished by a single diacritic stroke
whose rendering conventions differ between Indian and Nepali handwriting
traditions---a script-level domain gap that neither model resolves,
though \modelname{} handles the remaining classes substantially better.

\paragraph{Prashanth et al.\ 2021.}
After $32{\times}32$ resize, \modelname{} achieves \textbf{79.31\%} zero-shot on
this 22{,}500-image digit dataset~\cite{prashanth2021}; the baseline achieves \textbf{72.60\%}.
Both results are consistent with the NHCD digit outcome and the CMATERdb result from Section~\ref{sec:transfer}.
Devanagari digit~5 confuses with digit~7 at high rates for both models, reflecting a genuine visual similarity in the script.

Matched in-distribution accuracy conceals a substantial generalisation gap.
Both out-of-distribution evaluations consistently favour \modelname{}---as does the corruption-robustness result from Section~\ref{sec:robustness}---suggesting that knowledge distillation from a diverse teacher ensemble provides a generalisation benefit that raw accuracy on a saturated benchmark cannot detect.
