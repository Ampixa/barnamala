\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

DHCD has reached the point where a larger recognizer no longer buys a
better recognizer.  At current resolution, the benchmark is saturated:
the remaining mistakes are governed more by label noise and handwriting
ambiguity than by model capacity.  In that setting, compactness is not a
compromise.  It is the right operating point.

\modelname{} (C1) makes the case concretely: \textbf{1.11\,M} parameters,
\textbf{99.73\,\%} accuracy, and statistical parity with the leading
large-model baseline (McNemar $p = 0.345$), even without knowledge
distillation.
Rigorous paired evaluation (C2) then exposes why no model can pull ahead:
the same 11-image intrinsic floor defeats every configuration tested,
large teacher ensembles included.
Ablations (C3) close the obvious escape routes: distillation target
cleanliness, ensemble size, and test-time augmentation produce no
statistically significant gain once sampling noise is controlled.
Efficiency profiling (C4) gives the practical upshot: a 15.6$\times$
reduction in parameters and memory and a 9.5$\times$ CPU-latency
reduction at no accuracy cost, with fully reproducible artifacts
released (C5).

The lesson for Devanagari recognition is therefore not to keep polishing
architectures against DHCD, but to measure more honestly and move to
harder evidence.  For that reason, \emph{paired evaluation protocols}
(McNemar or similar) should be standard before gains of a few hundredths
of a percent are treated as progress.  The field also needs
\emph{fresh, harder benchmarks}: higher-resolution scans, genuinely
independent writer pools, or multi-script character inventories that do
not share DHCD's provenance and can separate models that DHCD can no
longer separate.
