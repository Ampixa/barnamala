\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The saturation conclusion is uncomfortable but hard to avoid. At current
resolution, DHCD's remaining mistakes are governed more by label noise and
handwriting ambiguity than by model capacity---which means throwing a larger
recognizer at the problem no longer buys a better one. Compactness stops
being a compromise at that point. It becomes the right operating point.

\modelname{} reaches \textbf{99.73\,\%} accuracy with just \textbf{1.11\,M}
parameters, achieving statistical parity with the leading large-model baseline
(McNemar $p = 0.345$), even before knowledge distillation enters the picture:
a 15.6$\times$ reduction in parameters and a 9.5$\times$ CPU-latency reduction
at no accuracy cost. Why can't anything pull ahead? The same 11-error
intrinsic floor defeats every configuration tested---large teacher ensembles
included---and ablations close the obvious escape routes: distillation target
cleanliness, ensemble size, and test-time augmentation all produce no
statistically significant gain. The floor is the ceiling.

Two boundaries should be stated plainly. The saturation claim is tied to
DHCD's particular conditions---$32{\times}32$ resolution, concentrated label
noise, and its specific writer pool; a cleaner or higher-resolution benchmark
might expose capacity differences DHCD can no longer reveal. Transfer results
cover only the \textbf{digit subset} (10 of 46 classes): no independent
consonant target set sharing DHCD's label taxonomy exists, so consonant-level
transferability is out of scope here.

The practical implication for Devanagari recognition is to measure more
honestly before claiming progress. Gains of a few hundredths of a percent
mean nothing without \emph{paired evaluation}---McNemar or equivalent---against
the specific test cases where the compared model also fails. The benchmark
question is harder: DHCD has run its course as a discriminator.
Higher-resolution scans or genuinely independent writer pools are the next
necessary step.
