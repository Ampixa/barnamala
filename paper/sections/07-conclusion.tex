\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The results show that a \textbf{1.11\,M}-parameter compact student,
\modelname{}, matches the 17.32\,M-parameter MallaNet~\cite{malla2025} at
statistical parity on DHCD (McNemar $p = 0.345$, C1), even without
knowledge distillation.  Paired evaluation (C2) reveals an 11-image
intrinsic floor shared by every model tested, including large teacher
ensembles.  At current resolution, DHCD behaves as a saturated benchmark,
not as a task with room for meaningful architectural progress.  Ablations
(C3) confirm that no lever — distillation target cleanliness, ensemble
size, or TTA — produces a statistically significant gain.  Efficiency
profiling (C4) shows a 15.6$\times$ reduction in parameters and memory and
a 9.5$\times$ CPU-latency reduction at no accuracy cost within the
saturation regime, and full reproducible artifacts are released (C5).

When error reduction is bounded by label noise and handwriting ambiguity
rather than model capacity, compactness is not a compromise — it is the
appropriate design choice.  An order-of-magnitude smaller and faster
model incurs no accuracy penalty precisely because the remaining errors
are irreducible by any model.

Two directions merit priority in future work.  First, \emph{paired
evaluation protocols} (McNemar or similar) should become standard, so
that accuracy gains of a few hundredths of a percent are not reported
without a significance test.  Second, Devanagari recognition needs
\emph{fresh, harder benchmarks}: higher-resolution scans, genuinely
independent writer pools, or multi-script character inventories that do
not share DHCD's provenance and can discriminate between models that
DHCD can no longer separate.
