\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The saturation conclusion is uncomfortable but hard to avoid.  At current
resolution, DHCD's remaining mistakes are governed more by label noise and
handwriting ambiguity than by model capacity---which means throwing a larger
recognizer at the problem no longer buys a better one.  Compactness stops
being a compromise at that point.  It becomes the right operating point.

That reframing has practical weight.  \modelname{} reaches \textbf{99.73\,\%}
accuracy with just \textbf{1.11\,M} parameters, achieving statistical parity
with the leading large-model baseline (McNemar $p = 0.345$), even before
knowledge distillation enters the picture.  Efficiency profiling puts the
upshot in concrete terms: a 15.6$\times$ reduction in parameters and memory
and a 9.5$\times$ CPU-latency reduction at no accuracy cost, with fully
reproducible artifacts released.

Why can't anything pull ahead?  Paired evaluation exposes the answer.
The same 11-image intrinsic floor defeats every configuration tested---large
teacher ensembles included---and the ablations close the obvious escape
routes.  Distillation target cleanliness, ensemble size, and test-time
augmentation produce no statistically significant gain once sampling noise
is controlled.  Scaling the teacher pool from 9 to 15 changes nothing
outside the significance frontier.  The floor is the ceiling.

For Devanagari recognition, the implication is not to keep polishing
architectures against DHCD, but to measure more honestly and move to
harder evidence.  \emph{Paired evaluation protocols} (McNemar or similar)
should be standard before gains of a few hundredths of a percent are
treated as progress.  The field also needs \emph{fresh, harder benchmarks}:
higher-resolution scans, genuinely independent writer pools, or multi-script
character inventories that do not share DHCD's provenance and can separate
models that DHCD can no longer separate.
