\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The saturation conclusion is uncomfortable but hard to avoid.  At current
resolution, DHCD's remaining mistakes are governed more by label noise and
handwriting ambiguity than by model capacity---which means throwing a larger
recognizer at the problem no longer buys a better one.  Compactness stops
being a compromise at that point.  It becomes the right operating point.

\modelname{} reaches \textbf{99.73\,\%} accuracy with just \textbf{1.11\,M}
parameters, achieving statistical parity with the leading large-model baseline
(McNemar $p = 0.345$), even before knowledge distillation enters the picture:
a 15.6$\times$ reduction in parameters and a 9.5$\times$ CPU-latency reduction
at no accuracy cost.  Why can't anything pull ahead?  The same 11-image
intrinsic floor defeats every configuration tested---large teacher ensembles
included---and ablations close the obvious escape routes: distillation target
cleanliness, ensemble size, and test-time augmentation all produce no
statistically significant gain.  The floor is the ceiling.

Two limitations are worth noting.  The saturation claim is tied to DHCD's
particular conditions---$32{\times}32$ resolution, concentrated label noise,
and its writer pool; a cleaner or higher-resolution benchmark might reveal
capacity differences DHCD can no longer expose.  Transfer results are limited
to the \textbf{digit subset} (10 of 46 classes): no independent consonant
target set with DHCD's label taxonomy exists, so consonant-level
transferability remains untested.

For Devanagari recognition the implication is to measure more honestly and move
to harder evidence.  \emph{Paired evaluation protocols} (McNemar or similar)
should be standard before gains of a few hundredths of a percent are treated as
progress.  The field needs \emph{fresh, harder benchmarks}: higher-resolution
scans, genuinely independent writer pools, or multi-script inventories that can
separate models DHCD no longer can.
