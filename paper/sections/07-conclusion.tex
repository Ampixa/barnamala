\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The results show that a \textbf{1.11\,M}-parameter compact student,
\modelname{}, matches the 17.32\,M-parameter MallaNet~\cite{malla2025} at
statistical parity on DHCD (McNemar $p = 0.345$, C1), even without
knowledge distillation.  Paired evaluation (C2) reveals an 11-image
intrinsic floor shared by every model tested, including large teacher
ensembles.  At current resolution, DHCD behaves as a saturated benchmark,
not as a task with room for meaningful architectural progress.  Ablations
(C3) confirm that no lever — distillation target cleanliness, ensemble
size, or TTA — produces a statistically significant gain.  Efficiency
profiling (C4) shows a 15.6$\times$ size and latency advantage at no
accuracy cost within the saturation regime, and full reproducible
artifacts are released (C5).

At saturation, compact models are not merely adequate; they are the
appropriate tool.  When error reduction is bounded by label noise and
handwriting ambiguity rather than model capacity, an order-of-magnitude
smaller and faster model carries no penalty.

Future work should move in two directions.  First, \emph{paired
evaluation protocols} (McNemar or similar) should become standard, so
that accuracy gains of a few hundredths of a percent are not reported
without a significance test.  Second, Devanagari needs \emph{fresh,
harder benchmarks}: higher-resolution scans, genuinely independent writer
pools, or multi-script character inventories that do not share DHCD's
provenance and can distinguish models that DHCD can no longer separate.
