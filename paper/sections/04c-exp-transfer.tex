\subsection{DHCD$\to$CMATERdb Digit Transfer}
\label{sec:transfer}

We test whether the DHCD-trained distilled student has learned digit
structure or the visual habits of one collection pipeline by evaluating
its representations on CMATERdb~3.2.1 \cite{das2012cmaterdb}, an
independently collected dataset of handwritten Devanagari \emph{numerals}
(digits 0--9; 10 classes).  CMATERdb changes the scanner workflow,
writers, paper stocks, and binarisation pipeline, so the overlapping
labels arrive from a visibly different source distribution.

\paragraph{Provenance guard.}
We first rule out accidental overlap: before evaluation, we check that no
CMATERdb image appears in the DHCD training split.  Perceptual hashes
(pHash) are computed for all CMATERdb samples and compared with 20,000
DHCD digit references using Hamming distance.  This yields
\textbf{0 near-duplicate pairs at a threshold of $\leq$5\,bits}; the
minimum observed Hamming distance is \textbf{6\,bits}.  With contamination ruled
out, the transfer result reflects domain shift rather than memorisation.

\paragraph{Polarity alignment.}
The remaining setup difference is photometric: CMATERdb stores black ink
on a white background, the inverse of DHCD's white-on-black convention.
An automatic polarity inversion is applied to all CMATERdb images before
evaluation so foreground intensity matches training; the step is uniform
across the dataset and is not dataset-specific tuning.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:transfer} (upper rows) summarises the transfer outcomes.
The most stringent test is \emph{zero-shot}: the DHCD-trained distilled
student is applied directly to CMATERdb, with no CMATERdb training images,
and achieves \textbf{76.6$\pm$3.7\%} accuracy.  A supervised baseline
trained on DHCD digit images \emph{only} with the same 10-class head
reaches \textbf{62.7\%}.  The models are statistically tied on the
in-distribution DHCD test set, but once the scanner, writers, paper, and
binarisation change, knowledge distillation carries a
\textbf{+14 percentage points} advantage into CMATERdb.

A \emph{linear probe} localises that advantage.  With the distilled
backbone frozen and only a fresh 10-class linear head trained on CMATERdb,
accuracy improves to \textbf{85.4\%}, showing that the frozen features
already support a small CMATERdb-specific head.  Light full
\emph{fine-tuning} (30 epochs on CMATERdb with a low learning rate)
reaches \textbf{97.8\%}, the upper end once the whole model can adapt.

The interpretation is deliberately narrow.  This experiment covers only
the \textbf{digit subset} (10 of 46 DHCD classes); it does not evaluate
transfer of the full Devanagari character inventory.  Within that scope,
after contamination and polarity controls, the distilled student preserves
substantially more useful digit structure under dataset shift than the
supervised DHCD-only baseline.

\subsection{Corruption Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

Out-of-distribution robustness is measured with additive Gaussian
\emph{noise}, Gaussian \emph{blur}, and \emph{contrast} reduction.  Each
is applied at five severity levels (following the ImageNet-C protocol
adapted to $32{\times}32$ greyscale images).  We report mean accuracy
across severities 1--5 (mCA) for each corruption family and overall.

\paragraph{Fair evaluation protocol.}
Each model uses its \emph{own} training-time normalisation on the same
corrupted $[0,1]$ input images.  For \modelname{} this is the learned
per-channel statistics from DHCD training; for MallaNet it is the fixed
mean/std of $(0.5, 0.5)$ reported in \cite{malla2025}.  The corrupted
inputs are identical; only the normalisation differs, keeping the
comparison focused on robustness rather than an asymmetric preprocessing
advantage.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:transfer} (lower rows) shows the outcome; Figure~\ref{fig:robust}
plots accuracy by severity.  Robustness is \emph{not} uniform across
corruption types.

\begin{table}[t]\centering\caption{Transfer and corruption robustness. mCA = mean accuracy over severities 1--5.}\label{tab:transfer}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}\toprule
Metric & \modelname & MallaNet \\\midrule
CMATERdb zero-shot (distilled) & 76.6\% & n/a \\
CMATERdb fine-tuned & 97.8\% & n/a \\
Clean DHCD & 99.75\% & 99.71\% \\
mCA noise & 54.7\% & 66.9\% \\
mCA blur & 97.0\% & 37.6\% \\
mCA contrast & 75.5\% & 11.5\% \\
mCA overall & \textbf{75.7\%} & 38.7\% \\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/robustness_curves.pdf}
  \caption{Per-severity accuracy under noise, blur, and contrast corruptions.
    \modelname{} (solid) maintains high accuracy under blur and contrast at all
    severities; MallaNet (dashed) collapses under blur and contrast but is
    more robust to additive noise.}
  \label{fig:robust}
\end{figure}

\modelname{} is approximately \textbf{2$\times$} more robust overall
(mCA 75.7\% vs.\ 38.7\%) and leads by wide margins under \emph{blur}
(97.0\% vs.\ 37.6\%) and \emph{contrast} reduction (75.5\% vs.\ 11.5\%).
MallaNet's collapse under these corruptions likely reflects two factors:
its high-frequency convolutional (HFC) design amplifies blur, and its
fixed $(0.5, 0.5)$ normalisation is sensitive to global intensity shifts
introduced by contrast corruption.

\modelname{} is \textbf{not} uniformly superior.  Under \emph{additive
noise}, MallaNet is more robust (mCA 66.9\% vs.\ 54.7\%), suggesting that
MallaNet's larger capacity and fixed normalisation provide some advantage
when the corruption is high-frequency rather than low-frequency.  We do
not claim an across-the-board robustness win; the relative ordering is
corruption-type-dependent and neither model is dominated in every respect.
Overall mCA favours \modelname{} ($+37.0$ pts), but the noise result is a
clear exception for noise-heavy deployment environments.
