\subsection{DHCD to CMATERdb Digit Transfer}
\label{sec:transfer}

Does the distilled student learn digit structure, or just the visual habits of
one collection pipeline?  We probe this by running its representations on
CMATERdb~3.2.1 \cite{das2012cmaterdb}---an independently collected set of
handwritten Devanagari \emph{numerals} (digits 0--9; 10 classes).  CMATERdb
changes the scanner workflow, writers, paper stocks, and binarisation pipeline,
so the overlapping labels arrive from a visibly different source distribution.

\paragraph{Provenance guard.}
Before anything else, we check that no CMATERdb image appears in the DHCD
training split---a mundane but necessary step.  Perceptual hashes (pHash) are
computed for all CMATERdb samples and compared with 20,000 DHCD digit
references using Hamming distance.  The result: \textbf{0 near-duplicate pairs
at a threshold of $\leq$5\,bits}, with a minimum observed distance of
\textbf{6\,bits}.  Contamination ruled out, the transfer numbers reflect domain
shift rather than memorisation.

\paragraph{Polarity alignment.}
CMATERdb stores black ink on a white background---the inverse of DHCD's
white-on-black convention.  We apply a uniform polarity inversion to all
CMATERdb images before evaluation so foreground intensity matches training.
This is preprocessing, not tuning; every image gets the same treatment.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:transfer} (upper rows) summarises the transfer outcomes.
The sharpest test is \emph{zero-shot}: the DHCD-trained distilled student
applied directly to CMATERdb, no CMATERdb training images, achieves
\textbf{76.6$\pm$3.7\%} accuracy.  A supervised baseline trained on DHCD
digit images \emph{only} with the same 10-class head reaches \textbf{62.7\%}.
On the in-distribution DHCD test set the two are statistically tied---but once
the scanner, writers, paper, and binarisation change, knowledge distillation
carries a \textbf{+14 percentage points} advantage into CMATERdb.

A \emph{linear probe} localises where that advantage sits.  Freezing the
distilled backbone and training only a fresh 10-class linear head on CMATERdb
raises accuracy to \textbf{85.4\%}; the frozen features apparently already
support a small CMATERdb-specific head without any backbone adjustment.
Light full \emph{fine-tuning} (30 epochs on CMATERdb with a low learning rate)
reaches \textbf{97.8\%}, the natural ceiling once the whole model can adapt.

A scope note: this experiment covers only the \textbf{digit subset} (10 of
46 DHCD classes) and says nothing about transfer of the full Devanagari
character inventory.  Within that scope---after contamination and polarity
controls---the distilled student preserves substantially more useful digit
structure under dataset shift than the supervised DHCD-only baseline.

\subsection{Corruption Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

Out-of-distribution robustness is measured with additive Gaussian
\emph{noise}, Gaussian \emph{blur}, and \emph{contrast} reduction.  Each
is applied at five severity levels (following the ImageNet-C protocol
adapted to $32{\times}32$ greyscale images).  We report mean accuracy
across severities 1--5 (mCA) for each corruption family and overall.

\paragraph{Fair evaluation protocol.}
Each model uses its \emph{own} training-time normalisation on the same
corrupted $[0,1]$ input images.  For \modelname{} this is the learned
per-channel statistics from DHCD training; for MallaNet it is the fixed
mean/std of $(0.5, 0.5)$ reported in \cite{malla2025}.  The corrupted
inputs are identical; only the normalisation differs, keeping the
comparison focused on robustness rather than an asymmetric preprocessing
advantage.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:transfer} (lower rows) shows the outcome; Figure~\ref{fig:robust}
plots accuracy by severity.  Robustness is \emph{not} uniform across
corruption types.

\begin{table}[t]\centering\caption{Transfer and corruption robustness. mCA = mean accuracy over severities 1--5.}\label{tab:transfer}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}\toprule
Metric & \modelname & MallaNet \\\midrule
CMATERdb zero-shot (distilled) & 76.6\% & n/a \\
CMATERdb fine-tuned & 97.8\% & n/a \\
Clean DHCD & 99.75\% & 99.71\% \\
mCA noise & 54.7\% & 66.9\% \\
mCA blur & 97.0\% & 37.6\% \\
mCA contrast & 75.5\% & 11.5\% \\
mCA overall & \textbf{75.7\%} & 38.7\% \\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/robustness_curves.pdf}
  \caption{Per-severity accuracy under noise, blur, and contrast corruptions.
    \modelname{} (solid) maintains high accuracy under blur and contrast at all
    severities; MallaNet (dashed) collapses under blur and contrast but is
    more robust to additive noise.}
  \label{fig:robust}
\end{figure}

\modelname{} is approximately \textbf{2$\times$} more robust overall
(mCA 75.7\% vs.\ 38.7\%), and the blur and contrast gaps are striking:
97.0\% vs.\ 37.6\% under \emph{blur}, 75.5\% vs.\ 11.5\% under
\emph{contrast} reduction.  MallaNet's collapse here arguably reflects two
compounding factors---its high-frequency convolutional (HFC) design amplifies
blur, and the fixed $(0.5, 0.5)$ normalisation turns out to be sensitive to
the global intensity shifts that contrast corruption introduces.

One clear reversal: under \emph{additive noise}, MallaNet is the more robust
model (mCA 66.9\% vs.\ 54.7\%).  It seems that MallaNet's larger capacity and
fixed normalisation offer some advantage specifically when the corruption is
high-frequency rather than low-frequency.  Overall mCA favours \modelname{}
($+37.0$ pts), but practitioners targeting noise-heavy deployment environments
should note that the noise ranking goes the other way.
